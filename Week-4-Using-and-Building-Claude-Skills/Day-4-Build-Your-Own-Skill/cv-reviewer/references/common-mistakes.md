# Common Resume Mistakes

A checklist to sanity-check against before finalizing any review — if the report doesn't mention any of the mistakes actually present in the document, the review isn't thorough enough.

## Content Mistakes

- **Listing duties instead of achievements.** "Managed social media accounts" vs. "Grew Instagram following from 2K to 40K in 6 months through a redesigned content calendar."
- **No quantification anywhere.** Every bullet describes activity, none describe scale or result.
- **Generic objective/summary statements.** "Seeking a challenging position where I can grow" says nothing employer-specific and wastes prime space.
- **Irrelevant or outdated content crowding the page.** A high-school achievement on a resume 10 years into a career; an unrelated retail job given equal weight to the most recent, relevant role.
- **Buzzword overuse without substance.** "Synergistic team player leveraging cutting-edge solutions" — reads as filler because it's not tied to anything concrete.
- **Exaggeration or fabrication.** Titles, dates, or scope that don't match reality — beyond being dishonest, inconsistencies are a common reason candidates fail reference/background checks after an offer.
- **Missing or buried keywords relevant to the target role**, especially when a job description is available and the resume doesn't reflect its terminology at all.

## Structural Mistakes

- **Missing standard sections** — no Skills section, no Education, no dates.
- **Non-chronological or unclear ordering** within a section.
- **Inconsistent formatting** — mixed fonts, inconsistent bullet styles, inconsistent bolding conventions.
- **Wrong length for career stage** — a 3-page resume for 2 years of experience; a cramped 1-pager for a 15-year career that needs 2.
- **"References available upon request."** Universally assumed; wastes a line.
- **Orphaned lines / awkward page breaks** that split a job entry across a page boundary.

## Language & Grammar Mistakes

- **Typos and spelling errors** — the single fastest way to get discarded by a careful reader.
- **Inconsistent verb tense** — mixing past and present within the same role, or present tense for a past role.
- **First-person pronouns** ("I led," "my team") — bullets should drop the implied subject.
- **Passive voice** — "The migration was completed by the team" instead of "Led the team through the migration."
- **Inconsistent punctuation** — some bullets end with periods, others don't.
- **Sentence fragments that read as broken rather than intentional** — bullet-style fragments are expected; grammatically broken ones are not.

## ATS & Formatting Mistakes

- **Resume saved as a scanned image** with no extractable text layer.
- **Multi-column layouts** that scramble parsing order.
- **Contact info in a header/footer** that some parsers skip.
- **Critical content inside tables, text boxes, or graphics.**
- **Unusual date formats** or missing end dates.
- **Non-standard section headers** ("My Story" instead of "Experience").
- **Unprofessional file name** (e.g., `resume_final_v3_USE THIS ONE.pdf`).

## Personal Branding Mistakes

- **Unprofessional email address** (nicknames, birth years, joke handles).
- **Personal information that invites bias** — age, marital status, photo — unless standard in the candidate's region/industry.
- **Hobbies/interests section that adds no signal** and crowds out substantive content (an exception: a hobby genuinely relevant to the role or that shows an unusual, relevant strength).
- **Overly casual tone** or excessive exclamation points that undercut a professional read.

## Seniority-Mismatch Mistakes

- **Junior candidate** using senior-level language disproportionate to actual scope ("Spearheaded company-wide strategic initiative" for a 3-month internship task).
- **Senior candidate** whose bullets read as entry-level task lists with no evidence of leadership, mentorship, or strategic scope, when the target role expects it.
- **No visible growth/progression** across roles for a candidate with a long work history — every job description reads the same level of scope.

## Gaps and Transitions

- **Unexplained gaps handled by silence** where a one-line, confident framing would remove ambiguity (e.g., "Career break for full-time caregiving, 2022-2023" is far better than an unexplained year-long hole).
- **Career changers who don't bridge the narrative** — no summary or framing connecting prior experience to the new target role, leaving the reader to guess at relevance.
